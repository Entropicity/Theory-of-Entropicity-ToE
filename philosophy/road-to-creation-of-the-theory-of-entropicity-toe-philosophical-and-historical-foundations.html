On the Monistic Philosophical Foundation of Obidi's Theory of Entropicity (ToE) and Its Physical Implications

The Theory of Entropicity (ToE), developed by John Onimisi Obidi, is grounded in a radical ontological shift that elevates entropy from a secondary statistical descriptor to the fundamental substrate of physical reality. Rather than treating entropy as a derivative measure of disorder, ToE positions it as the primary field from which all physical structures and laws emerge. This monistic philosophical foundation reshapes the conceptual landscape of physics, replacing traditional primitives with a single, universal entropic field. The following sections articulate the core pillars of this worldview and their implications for time, causality, geometry, information, and the self-organizing nature of the universe.

1. Ontological Monism: Entropy as the Primordial Field

At the heart of ToE lies the assertion that entropy is a universal physical field, denoted by the scalar field:

\( S(x) \)

This field is not a statistical abstraction but the primary ontological entity from which all physical phenomena arise. ToE reverses the conventional hierarchy of physics:

The “Light Source” vs. The Shadow: Traditional physics treats entropy as a shadow—a passive record of the degradation of matter and energy. ToE inverts this relationship, asserting that the entropic field is the light source that generates matter, space, time, and all observable structure. Physical reality becomes the emergent “shadow” cast by the dynamics of entropy.

Unified Substrate: ToE replaces the traditional primitives of physics—spacetime, quantum states, and fundamental forces—with a single entropic field. All physical laws are reinterpreted as emergent manifestations of the dynamics, gradients, and curvature of \( S(x) \).

2. The Nature of Time and Causality

ToE provides a first‑principles derivation of the arrow of time and the limits of causality, moving beyond geometric or statistical interpretations.

Chronos (Entropic Flow): Time is not an independent dimension or coordinate. Instead, it is the irreversible flow of the entropic field. The past corresponds to accumulated entropy, while the future represents unrealized entropic potential.

The No‑Rush Theorem (NRT): This principle states that no physical interaction can occur instantaneously. Every change requires a finite duration for the entropic field to reconfigure itself. NRT establishes a universal lower bound on the rate of physical processes.

The Speed of Light as an Entropic Limit: The universal constant \( c \) is reinterpreted as the maximum rate of entropic reconfiguration. Rather than being an arbitrary postulate, the speed of light becomes a thermodynamic necessity arising from the finite responsiveness of the entropic field.

3. Emergent Physical Phenomena

Within the entropic worldview, familiar physical constructs are redefined as emergent consequences of entropy dynamics:

Space: Space is not a pre‑existing container but a map of entropic gradients and distributions. Spatial structure reflects the configuration of the entropic field.

Gravity: Gravity is not a fundamental force. It emerges from entropic curvature as the field seeks equilibrium. Gravitational attraction becomes a manifestation of entropic gradients.

Mass and Inertia: Mass is reinterpreted as localized resistance to entropic reconfiguration. Inertia reflects the entropic “cost” of altering a system’s configuration.

Quantum Uncertainty: Quantum uncertainty arises from the finite‑time, entropically constrained process of state transition. This provides a dynamical basis for wavefunction collapse and replaces probabilistic postulates with entropic geometry.

4. Information‑Geometric Foundation

ToE employs Information Geometry to unify statistical probability with physical reality.

Obidi Curvature Invariant (OCI): The theory identifies a minimum distinguishable curvature gap in the entropic field:

\( \Delta R_{\min} = \ln 2 \)

This invariant establishes a fundamental scale for reality, where existence itself requires a quantifiable entropic expenditure. Distinguishability becomes a geometric threshold.

Isomorphism: ToE posits that physical geometry and informational geometry are isomorphic through the entropic field. Mathematical constructs such as the Fisher–Rao metric acquire direct physical meaning.

5. Algorithmic and Self‑Organizing Universe

ToE envisions the universe as an autonomous, self‑organizing system that “computes” its own state through local entropic exchanges.

The Obidi Action: Analogous to the Einstein–Hilbert action, the Obidi Action is a variational principle that dictates that the universe follows paths optimizing entropic flow while minimizing internal constraint.

Iterative Solutions: Unlike the closed‑form equations of General Relativity, ToE’s field equations are inherently iterative. They mirror the way information is updated in Bayesian inference, reflecting the universe’s continuous process of entropic self‑revision.

7. Additional Philosophical Commitments of the Theory of Entropicity (ToE)

The Theory of Entropicity extends its monistic foundation into several broader philosophical commitments:

Ontological Status of Entropy: Entropy is elevated to a primary, continuous, and dynamic field \( S(x) \), not a secondary abstraction.

Fundamental Emergence: All physical reality—including gravity, motion, and spacetime—is an emergent property of entropy gradients.

Irreversibility as Fundamental: The arrow of time and causality arise from the irreversible flow of entropy.

Informational Realism: Information is treated as a physical, entropic entity linking thermodynamics and quantum mechanics.

Finite Entropic Limitation: The speed of light \( c \) is the maximum rate at which entropy can rearrange.

Entropic Consciousness (Radical Hypothesis): Consciousness may be modeled as a self‑referential entropic loop—a complex internal and external entropy exchange.
